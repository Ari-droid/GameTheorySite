Welcome to Game Theory 101

Understanding Strategy, Choices, and Decision-Making

A Concept Over 70 Years Old

Originated in the 1940s with John von Neumann & Oskar Morgenstern

Game theory began as a mathematical exploration of strategic behavior in competitive environments. In 1944, John von Neumann and economist Oskar Morgenstern published "Theory of Games and Economic Behavior," a foundational text that formalized the field. Their work examined how rational players should act when their choices depend on the actions of others.

The Prisoner's Dilemma: The Classic Game

The Prisoner's Dilemma reveals how two individuals acting in their own self-interest may lead to worse outcomes than if they cooperated. Two prisoners are offered a deal: betray the other and go free while the other serves time, or stay silent and risk a harsher sentence. The paradox? Mutual betrayal is the rational strategy, but mutual cooperation would benefit both more.

The Ultimatum Game: Fairness in Action

In this game, one player proposes how to divide a sum of money; the other can accept or reject the offer. If rejected, both get nothing. While rational choice predicts accepting any non-zero offer, in practice, unfair proposals are often rejected. The game highlights how fairness, emotion, and social norms affect decision-making.

The Nash Equilibrium: The Balance of Strategy

Named after mathematician John Nash, a Nash Equilibrium occurs when no player can benefit by changing their strategy while others keep theirs unchanged. It represents a stable state in strategic games and is fundamental in economics, politics, and even biology. Not all games have a single Nash Equilibrium, and some have none in pure strategies.

Overview

Game theory is the study of strategic interaction among rational decision-makers. It provides tools to analyze situations where outcomes depend on the actions of multiple agents. Used in economics, biology, politics, and computer science, game theory helps explain and predict behavior in competitive and cooperative settings.

Case Studies

Cold War Deterrence: Game theory informed U.S. and Soviet strategies of mutually assured destruction.
Auction Design: Economists used game theory to design spectrum auctions, maximizing efficiency and revenue.
Biological Evolution: Strategies like altruism and aggression have been modeled using evolutionary game theory.
Market Competition: Firms use game-theoretic models to anticipate competitors' pricing and output decisions.

Impact & Legacy

Game theory has transformed economics and spread into many disciplines. It underpins modern economic theory, international diplomacy, behavioral psychology, and even artificial intelligence. With Nobel Prizes awarded to its pioneers, game theory continues to shape how we understand conflict, cooperation, and strategic thinking.
